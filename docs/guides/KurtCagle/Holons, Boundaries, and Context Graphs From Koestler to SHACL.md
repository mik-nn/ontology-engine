_Continuing my discussion on context graphs, one of the more vexing questions involved is a core one in systems theory: what is the distinction between an entity and a system? I believe this is a core question when dealing with context graphs, and as such it relates to understanding both the world of Arthur Koestler and one of his most influential theories: the **holon**._

## Arthur Koestler and the Holon

Arthur Koestler is not the most obvious source for a theory that would eventually influence computer science, knowledge engineering, and organisational design. Born in Budapest in 1905, he was primarily known as a novelist and political journalist. His 1940 novel _Darkness at Noon_ — a devastating portrait of Stalinist show trials drawn from his own disillusionment with communism — remains one of the most important political novels of the twentieth century. He was a war correspondent, a prisoner under Franco, briefly interned by the British as an enemy alien, and one of the more restless and combative public intellectuals of his era.

The scientific work came later. In the 1960s and 1970s Koestler turned his attention to biology, psychology, and systems theory, convinced that the dominant frameworks in each field were missing something fundamental about how complex living systems actually work. The result was a series of increasingly ambitious books — _The Act of Creation_ (1964), _The Ghost in the Machine_ (1967), and _Janus: A Summing Up_ (1978) — in which he tried to articulate a general theory of hierarchical organisation in nature and mind. The holon was the centrepiece of that theory.

### The Problem Koestler Was Trying to Solve

To understand why the holon concept matters, it helps to understand what Koestler found inadequate about the alternatives available to him.

Biology and social science in the mid-twentieth century tended to oscillate between two poles. On one side was **atomism**: the idea that complex systems are best understood by decomposing them into their smallest parts. Understand the individual neuron and you understand the brain; understand the individual worker and you understand the organisation. On the other side was **holism**: the idea that the whole is greater than the sum of its parts, that you can only understand a system by studying it as an integrated unity, not by breaking it apart.

Koestler thought both positions were wrong — or rather that they were answers to the wrong question. The real structure of biological and social systems, he argued, is neither a collection of atomic parts nor an undifferentiated whole. It is a **nested hierarchy of entities that are simultaneously wholes and parts** — simultaneously self-contained and embedded. No level of this hierarchy is the “real” level; the cell is not more real than the organ, and the organ is not more real than the organism. Each level has its own coherence, its own rules, its own interior life — and each is simultaneously a component of something larger.

He needed a word for these entities — things that are irreducibly both wholes and parts — and he coined one. From the Greek _holos_ (whole) and the suffix _-on_ suggesting a particle or unit (as in proton or neutron), he constructed: **holon**.

### What a Holon Is

A holon is any entity that is, simultaneously and without contradiction, a whole in its own right and a part of something larger.

Koestler’s examples ranged across biology, psychology, and social organisation. A cell is a holon: it is a complete living system with its own metabolism, its own regulatory mechanisms, its own interior coherence — and it is also a component of a tissue, which is a component of an organ, which is a component of an organism. None of these levels cancels out the others. The cell does not stop being a whole because it is part of a tissue.

The same structure appears in language. A word is a whole — it has meaning, it obeys grammatical rules, it can stand alone. It is also a part of a sentence, which is a part of a paragraph, which is a part of a text. A syllable is a whole with its own phonological rules, and a part of the word above it. You can analyse language at any of these levels, and each level has genuine explanatory purchase. None is the “right” level.

In social systems: a person is a whole — with their own psychology, values, and habits — and a part of a family, which is a part of a community, which is a part of a society. A team within a company is a whole, with its own culture and internal coordination, and a part of the department above it.

The key insight is that this is not just a description of how we happen to organise things for convenience. It is, Koestler argued, the _natural structure_ of complex adaptive systems. The nested hierarchy is not imposed from outside; it emerges from the dynamics of how complex systems self-organise under evolutionary and developmental pressure.

### The Two Faces of a Holon: Janus

Koestler named his last major work _Janus_ after the Roman god of doorways — the god with two faces, one looking inward and one looking outward. It was the perfect emblem for what he wanted to say about holons.

Every holon has two faces, and they pull in opposite directions.

The **self-assertive tendency** is the face that looks inward. It is the holon’s drive to maintain its own integrity, its own internal coherence, its own identity as a whole. A cell regulates its own chemistry. A team develops its own norms. A department guards its own budget and expertise. This is not selfishness or pathology — it is the necessary condition for the holon’s existence as a distinct entity at all. Without self-assertion, the holon dissolves into its surroundings and ceases to be a holon.

The **integrative tendency** is the face that looks outward. It is the holon’s capacity to function as a part — to subordinate its behaviour to the requirements of the larger whole it belongs to, to coordinate with other holons at the same level, to participate in the collective behaviour of the system above it. Without integration, the holon becomes a rogue element — cancerous, in Koestler’s vocabulary: growing without constraint, refusing coordination, eventually destroying the system it inhabits.

Healthy systems, Koestler argued, maintain a dynamic balance between these two tendencies. Too much self-assertion and the hierarchy fragments into warring parts. Too much integration and the parts lose their distinctiveness and the system becomes rigid, unable to adapt. The sweet spot — and it is always a dynamic equilibrium, never a fixed point — is what he called the holon in good working order.

This two-faced quality is why Janus was the right image. Every holon stands in a doorway: one face turned toward its own interior, one face turned toward the world outside. And the doorway — the boundary between interior and exterior — is not a wall but a membrane: selective, active, capable of deciding what passes in each direction.

### The Holarchy

Koestler used the term **holarchy** for the nested hierarchy of holons — deliberately distinguishing it from simple hierarchy. A conventional hierarchy is typically understood as a chain of command, with higher levels controlling lower ones. A holarchy is something subtler: each level has genuine autonomy (self-assertion), and coordination between levels is a negotiated, emergent property rather than top-down control.

In a holarchy, the levels do not simply override each other. Higher-level patterns constrain the range of behaviour available to lower-level holons — an organ constrains what its cells can do — but within those constraints the lower levels operate by their own rules. And information flows in both directions: upward, as local behaviour aggregates into higher-level patterns; and downward, as higher-level states create the boundary conditions for local behaviour. This bidirectional influence is what distinguishes a holarchy from a simple control hierarchy.

One practical implication Koestler drew from this: you cannot manage a complex holarchy from the top. Each level must have sufficient autonomy to handle disturbances at its own scale. An organisation in which every decision requires escalation to the highest level is not a holarchy functioning well — it is a holarchy with its integrative tendency pathologically overdeveloped, suppressing the self-assertion that makes each level capable of handling its own domain.

## From Philosophy to Architecture: Holons in Knowledge Systems

When Koestler developed these ideas, computing systems had nothing like the expressive power needed to implement a holarchic model. But the structural intuitions are exactly right for what modern knowledge graphs and semantic web technologies are trying to do.

A knowledge graph is not a flat collection of facts. It is — or should be — a nested structure in which different domains, organisations, and contexts each maintain their own coherent view of the world, expose selected aspects of that view to others, and receive information from others through validated boundary crossings. The problem of **contextual misalignment** — the fact that the same concept means subtly different things in different contexts — is precisely the problem Koestler was describing when he talked about the need for each holon to maintain its own interior coherence while participating in a shared higher-level system.

The holon concept gives us a vocabulary for talking about this that neither pure atomism nor pure holism can provide. We do not want to collapse all knowledge into one flat universal graph (integration without self-assertion — the loss of local coherence). We do not want each organisation to maintain a completely isolated silo with no connection to anything else (self-assertion without integration — the loss of the holarchy). We want holons: bounded, self-coherent knowledge contexts that participate in a larger system through well-defined, validated boundary interfaces.

The question then becomes concrete: in the actual machinery of RDF and SPARQL, what are the parts?

### A Four-Layer Architecture

The key move is to distinguish four concerns that superficially look like one thing but are structurally separable. Each takes the form of a named graph sharing a common root IRI — the holon’s persistent identity — while serving a distinct role.

**The interior graph** (`GRAPH <ex:HolonA>`) holds the holon’s full internal state. Triples here are locally asserted as true within the holon’s closed world. No external agent reads this directly. This is where ground assertions land after passing the boundary inward.

**The shapes graph** (`GRAPH <ex:HolonA-shapes>`) encodes the boundary in computational form. This is not a _description_ of the boundary — it is the executable boundary itself. It contains the SHACL shapes that determine what signals are permitted to cross in either direction: inbound shapes define what a signal must look like to become a ground assertion; outbound shapes define what the holon will expose to the outside. The boundary is not the edge of the named graph (that is a serialisation artifact); it is this shape graph.

**The projection graph** (`GRAPH <ex:HolonA-projection>`) is what the holon presents to the outside — a curated, validated view of its interior state, filtered by the boundary rules. Other holons never see the interior directly; they see the projection. This is the holon’s exterior in Koestler’s sense: the holon-as-part, showing only what it elects to show at this boundary crossing.

**The context graph** is the shared meta-layer that wraps all holons. This is where the records of boundary crossings live — owned by neither party to any given exchange, readable by any authorised party. It is the permanent audit trail of what happened at which boundary and when.

The graph name IRI threads all four layers together. As an IRI, `<ex:HolonA>` is the holon’s persistent identity: you can make statements _about_ it — coherence scores, validation status, relationships to other holons — anywhere in the system. As a graph name, it addresses the interior. These are two distinct roles that the RDF data model happens to support with a single string, which is exactly the right level of elegance.

### SHACL as the Boundary Made Executable

This is where Koestler’s membrane metaphor finds its technical form. SHACL — the Shapes Constraint Language — is the W3C standard for expressing rules about what RDF data must look like. In the holarchic knowledge graph model, the shapes graph is not auxiliary documentation. It _is_ the boundary.

A SHACL shape graph can do three things relevant here. It can **validate** incoming signals, rejecting anything that does not meet the boundary’s conditions. It can **derive** the projection graph from the interior, using SPARQL-based inference rules to filter and transform what the holon exposes. And it can **check** the interior’s own consistency, maintaining the coherence that self-assertion requires.

The result is that interior privacy is architectural, not policy-based. An external validator querying another holon’s state is only ever _given_ the projection as its data source. The interior is structurally inaccessible — not locked away by convention or access control, but simply not present in the query scope. This is Koestler’s membrane made literal: selective, active, and enforced by the structure of the system rather than by rules that someone might forget to apply.

## In Practice: A University Registration System

An abstract architecture only becomes convincing when you can see it work. Consider a university department — a setting everyone understands, and one that has natural interior/exterior tension built in.

The **Computer Science Department** is a holon. It knows everything about its courses internally: enrolment counts, waitlists, room bookings, instructor assignments, grade distributions. But a student registering for a course only ever sees the public catalogue — available seats, prerequisites, schedule. The department’s internal complexity is invisible to them.

A second holon: the **Student Records Office**. It holds student academic histories. When the CS Department needs to verify prerequisites, it reads the Records Office’s _projection_ — not its interior. Neither holon ever opens the other’s interior directly. Everything flows through boundary crossings, validated by SHACL, recorded as timestamped events.

### The Interior: Everything the Department Knows

```
GRAPH ex:dept-cs {

    ex:Course-CS301
        a cg:Course ;
        rdfs:label "Algorithms and Data Structures" ;
        cg:seatCapacity    30 ;
        cg:seatsEnrolled   28 ;      # internal truth
        cg:seatsWaitlisted  3 ;      # invisible externally
        cg:instructor       ex:Prof-Kovacs ;
        cg:room             ex:Room-B204 ;
        cg:prerequisite     ex:Course-CS201 .

    ex:Prof-Kovacs
        a prov:Agent ;
        rdfs:label "Dr. Éva Kovács" ;
        cg:salary  "4800000"^^xsd:integer ;   # never projected
        cg:officeHours "Tuesday 14:00–16:00" .
}
```

The salary triple exists in the interior. It will never appear in the projection. The boundary enforces this — not by convention, but by the shape of what the projection is allowed to contain.

### The Boundary: The Shapes Graph

The shapes graph has two jobs: validate inbound signals and define what the outbound projection may contain.

```
GRAPH ex:dept-cs-shapes {

    # What a registration signal must look like to cross the boundary inbound

    cgsh:RegistrationSignalShape
        a sh:NodeShape ;
        sh:targetClass cg:RegistrationSignal ;

        sh:property [
            sh:path    cg:requestingStudent ;
            sh:class   cg:Student ;
            sh:minCount 1 ; sh:maxCount 1 ;
            sh:message "Signal must identify exactly one student." ;
        ] ;

        # Read the prerequisite check from the Records Office projection —
        # not from its interior, which is structurally inaccessible.
        sh:sparql [
            sh:message "Student has not completed the required prerequisite." ;
            sh:select """
                SELECT $this WHERE {
                    $this cg:requestingStudent ?student .
                    $this cg:targetCourse      ?course .
                    ?course cg:prerequisite    ?prereq .
                    FILTER NOT EXISTS {
                        GRAPH ex:records-projection {
                            ?student cg:completedCourse ?prereq .
                        }
                    }
                }
            """ ;
        ] ;

        sh:sparql [
            sh:message "Course has no available seats." ;
            sh:select """
                SELECT $this WHERE {
                    $this cg:targetCourse ?course .
                    GRAPH ex:dept-cs {
                        ?course cg:seatsEnrolled ?enrolled .
                        ?course cg:seatCapacity  ?capacity .
                        FILTER ( ?enrolled >= ?capacity )
                    }
                }
            """ ;
        ] .

    # What the projection is allowed to contain — outbound boundary policy

    cgsh:ProjectedCourseShape
        a sh:NodeShape ;
        sh:targetClass cg:Course ;
        sh:closed true ;
        sh:ignoredProperties ( rdf:type ) ;

        sh:property [ sh:path rdfs:label        ] ;
        sh:property [ sh:path cg:seatsAvailable ] ;   # derived, not raw
        sh:property [ sh:path cg:prerequisite   ] ;
        sh:property [ sh:path cg:officeHours    ] .
        # seatsEnrolled, seatsWaitlisted, salary — absent → excluded from projection
}
```

Notice that the prerequisite check reads from `ex:records-projection`, not from `ex:records`. Holons only read each other’s projections. The boundary-as-SHACL pattern applies uniformly and recursively across the holarchy.

### The Projection: What the Outside World Sees

The projection is not manually maintained. It is derived by a SHACL inference rule that pulls from the interior and filters through the outbound shape. A student looking up CS301 sees exactly this:

```
GRAPH ex:dept-cs-projection {

    ex:Course-CS301
        a cg:Course ;
        rdfs:label        "Algorithms and Data Structures" ;
        cg:seatsAvailable  2 ;          # derived: capacity(30) − enrolled(28)
        cg:prerequisite   ex:Course-CS201 .

    ex:Prof-Kovacs
        cg:officeHours "Tuesday 14:00–16:00" .

    # seatsEnrolled, seatsWaitlisted, room, instructor, salary → not projected
}
```

The projection is always a _consequence_ of the interior and the boundary policy. It is never edited directly. Whenever the interior changes, the rule re-derives it.

### The Boundary Crossing: A Student Registers

A student, Anna Németh, sends a registration signal to the CS Department’s boundary:

```
ex:Signal-2026-03-08-001
    a cg:RegistrationSignal ;
    cg:requestingStudent  ex:Student-Nemeth-Anna ;
    cg:targetCourse       ex:Course-CS301 ;
    prov:generatedAtTime  "2026-03-08T10:14:00Z"^^xsd:dateTimeStamp .
```

The SHACL validator runs against `ex:dept-cs-shapes`. It checks: Is Anna a valid student? Has she completed CS201 — reading that fact from `ex:records-projection`, not from the Records Office interior? Are there available seats? All three pass. The signal is conformant.

The control plane now produces a **ContextEvent** — the permanent record of this boundary crossing — and writes the ground assertion to the interior:

```
# The boundary crossing record, written to the shared context graph

ex:Event-2026-03-08-001
    a cg:DataEvent ;
    cg:localId           "registration-cs301-nemeth-2026-03-08" ;
    prov:generatedAtTime "2026-03-08T10:14:01Z"^^xsd:dateTimeStamp ;
    cg:initiatedBy       ex:Student-Nemeth-Anna ;
    cg:producedAssertion ex:Course-CS301 ;

    cg:atBoundary [
        cg:boundaryId  ex:Boundary-CS-Registration ;
        cg:sourceHolon ex:Student-Nemeth-Anna ;
        cg:targetHolon ex:dept-cs ;
    ] ;

    cg:contextFacet [
        cg:measuredCoherence "1.0"^^xsd:decimal ;
        cg:resolutionStatus  cg:Resolved ;
    ] .

# The ground assertion, annotated with its provenance using RDF 1.2 syntax

ex:Course-CS301  cg:enrolledStudent  ex:Student-Nemeth-Anna
    {| cg:producedByEvent    ex:Event-2026-03-08-001 ;
       cg:assertedAt         "2026-03-08T10:14:01Z"^^xsd:dateTimeStamp ;
       cg:assertionAuthority ex:Student-Nemeth-Anna ;
       cg:assertionGraph     ex:dept-cs |} .
```

That annotated triple lands in `GRAPH ex:dept-cs`, incrementing the interior’s enrolment count from 28 to 29. The projection rule re-runs. `cg:seatsAvailable` drops from 2 to 1 in `GRAPH ex:dept-cs-projection`. The next student to look up CS301 sees updated availability — without ever touching the interior.

## Three Principles the Example Illustrates

The university scenario is deliberately familiar, but the three structural principles it demonstrates apply anywhere you need multiple knowledge contexts to cooperate without merging.

**The boundary is not the edge of the named graph.** The edge of `GRAPH ex:dept-cs` is a serialisation boundary — a line in a file. It has no behaviour. The _actual_ boundary is `GRAPH ex:dept-cs-shapes`: the set of SHACL shapes that determine what can cross, in which direction, under what conditions. A graph edge is a line; a SHACL shape graph is a gate with a rulebook. Koestler’s membrane is not the container wall — it is the selective permeability encoded in the shapes.

**Holons only read each other’s projections.** The prerequisite check in the boundary validator reads `GRAPH ex:records-projection`, not `GRAPH ex:records`. This is not a convention that someone might forget to follow — it is enforced by the fact that the validator is only _given_ the projection as its input. Interior privacy is architectural. This is Koestler’s self-assertive tendency made structurally concrete: the holon’s interior is simply not available to external agents, regardless of their intent.

**The context graph belongs to nobody.** The ContextEvent lives in a shared medium that neither the student nor the CS Department owns. It is the permanent record of what happened at the boundary, written by the control plane, readable by any authorised party: auditors, the holarchy, the Records Office updating its own interior in response. This is what makes the event trail traversable across holon boundaries without any single holon having global visibility — the integrative tendency of the holarchy expressed as an append-only log.

## Conclusion

Koestler died in 1983. His scientific ideas were never fully accepted by the mainstream of any single discipline — too psychological for biologists, too biological for psychologists, too philosophical for both. That interdisciplinary restlessness, the refusal to stay within any one level of the hierarchy, seems in retrospect entirely appropriate for the man who gave us the word for things that are always simultaneously more than one thing at once.

What he could not have anticipated is that his architectural intuition — the bounded, self-coherent entity with a selective membrane, embedded in a hierarchy of similar entities — would find its clearest technical implementation not in biology or psychology but in the machinery of the semantic web. Named graphs provide the containment. SHACL provides the boundary behaviour. ContextEvents provide the audit trail. And the holarchy concept provides the reason any of it matters: because real knowledge — like real biology, real language, and real organisations — is never flat.

It is nested, all the way down, and all the way up.
